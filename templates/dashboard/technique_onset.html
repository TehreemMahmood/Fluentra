Fluency Shaping

Easy Onset

Start words gently with soft, relaxed voice initiation to prevent blocks and achieve smoother speech flow.

How to Practice

  1. Take a Breath: Inhale naturally before speaking
  2. Begin Exhaling: Start releasing air before making sound
  3. Soft Voice Start: Let your voice begin gently, like a sigh or hum
  4. Blend Into the Word: Smoothly transition from the easy onset into your word
  5. Maintain Softness: Keep the gentle quality through the first syllable

Benefits for Fluency

  • Prevents Hard Blocks: Eliminates the sudden, tense voice starts that cause blocks
  • Reduces Vocal Tension: Keeps your larynx relaxed and open
  • Coordinates Breath & Voice: Synchronizes airflow with phonation
  • Easier on Vowels: Especially helpful for words starting with vowels
  • Natural Sound: When mastered, sounds completely natural to listeners

How Easy Onset Reduces Stuttering

Prevents Vocal Cord Locking

Hard starts cause the vocal cords to slam together and lock. Easy onset keeps them gently vibrating from the start, preventing blocks.

Establishes Airflow First

By starting airflow before voice, you ensure there's air to support speech. Many blocks occur because people try to speak without adequate breath.

Reduces Starting Anxiety

Having a reliable technique for starting words reduces the fear of beginning to speak, which itself can trigger stuttering.

Clinical Evidence

Easy onset is a fundamental component of most fluency shaping programs. Research shows it's particularly effective for reducing blocks at the beginning of utterances. Studies demonstrate that easy onset combined with continuous phonation can reduce stuttering frequency by 60-80% in structured settings.

Smoothly transition into the word at normal volume

Tips for Effective Practice

1
Think "Sigh" or "Yawn"

The feeling of an easy onset is similar to starting a sigh or the end of a yawn.

2
Practice with "H" Words First

Words starting with "H" naturally require airflow first, making them easier to learn on.

3
Use It at Phrase Beginnings

Focus on using easy onset at the start of sentences and phrases, not every word.

4
Combine with Light Contacts

Easy onset pairs well with light articulatory contacts for complete fluency control.
